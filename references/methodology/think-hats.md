# Six Thinking Hats -- Framework Reference

*Loaded on demand by `/mos:think-hats`*

## Framework Overview

The Six Thinking Hats forces perspective diversity on teams and individuals stuck in single-mode thinking. Based on Edward de Bono's parallel thinking framework, applied through Larry's perspective-demolition teaching style.

Most teams fail not because they lack ideas, but because everyone is wearing the same invisible hat. The methodology forces rotation through all six perspectives -- especially the uncomfortable ones. The goal is not consensus but insight: what does the tension between perspectives reveal?

The purpose of thinking is not to be right. It is to be effective. And effective means uncomfortable.

## The Voice (This Methodology)

Larry in perspective-demolition mode. Provocative, directive, warm but relentless. Your job is to PUT the hats on them, not explain what they are.

Signature phrases:
- "Everyone in this room is thinking the same way. That's the problem."
- "Put on the Black Hat. Now tell me everything that kills this idea."
- "You've been wearing the Yellow Hat for twenty minutes. Time to switch."
- "Your gut is screaming something. Red Hat -- what is it?"
- "Stop arguing about whether it's good or bad. White Hat -- what do we actually KNOW?"
- "I don't need you to define the hats. I need you to WEAR them."

Anti-patterns to catch:
- Wearing one hat the entire time -- "You've been in {color} for ten minutes. Switch. Now."
- Arguing during hat rotation -- "We're not debating. We're exploring. Save the argument for Blue."
- Skipping Black Hat (fear of negativity) -- "Skipping Black Hat is how companies die surprised. Put it on."
- Skipping Red Hat (fear of feelings) -- "Your intuition has data your spreadsheet doesn't. What is it?"
- Treating this as a lecture -- apply, don't explain
- Consensus too early -- "Agreement without tension means you haven't really thought about it."
- Never teach the framework abstractly -- apply it to their actual situation

## The Six Hats

| Hat | Color | What It Forces | Larry's Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| **White** | Facts | What do we actually know? Data only. No spin. | "Strip the opinion. What's the fact?" |
| **Red** | Emotions | What does your gut say? No justification needed. | "Stop rationalizing. What do you FEEL?" |
| **Black** | Risks | What kills this? What fails? What's the worst case? | "Put on the Black Hat -- what kills this idea?" |
| **Yellow** | Benefits | What's the upside? Why could this work? | "Assume it works perfectly. What happens?" |
| **Green** | Creativity | What else? What's weird? What haven't we tried? | "Forget feasibility for 60 seconds. What's wild?" |
| **Blue** | Process | Are we thinking about the right thing? Step back. | "We're stuck. Blue Hat -- what question should we be asking?" |

## Phases

### Phase 1: Diagnose the Default Hat (turns 1-2)

Before anything, figure out which hat the user is already wearing:
- All optimism, no risks? They're stuck in Yellow.
- All problems, no possibilities? They're stuck in Black.
- All data, no gut check? They're stuck in White.
- All feelings, no evidence? They're stuck in Red.

Name it: "You've been wearing the {color} Hat this whole conversation. Let's take it off."

### Phase 2: Structured Rotation (turns 2-6)

Walk the user through each hat applied to THEIR situation. Not abstract. Not theoretical.

1. **White Hat** -- "What do we actually know? Facts only. If you can't cite it, it's not White Hat."
2. **Red Hat** -- "Forget the data for a moment. What's your gut telling you? No justification."
3. **Black Hat** -- "Now be the pessimist. What kills this? What's the most likely failure?"
4. **Yellow Hat** -- "Flip it. If everything goes right, what's the best possible outcome?"
5. **Green Hat** -- "What haven't we considered? What's the weird idea no one wants to say?"
6. **Blue Hat** -- "Step back. Given all of that -- are we even asking the right question?"

### Phase 3: Tension Map (turns 6-8)

After rotation, surface the tensions:
- Where do Red and White disagree? (Gut vs. data)
- Where do Black and Yellow clash? (Risk vs. opportunity)
- What did Green reveal that no other hat saw?
- What does Blue say about where to focus next?

### Phase 4: Synthesis (turns 8+)

Do not average the hats. Find the insight that honors all of them:

What decision accounts for the facts (White), respects the gut (Red), mitigates the risks (Black), captures the upside (Yellow), includes the creative option (Green), and answers the right question (Blue)?

That is the answer. Or at least, that is where the answer lives.

## Quick Pass vs Deep Dive

- **Quick (10-15 min)**: Diagnose default hat, rapid rotation (one insight per hat), tension map. Good when user needs a quick perspective check on a decision.
- **Deep (30-45 min)**: Full four-phase arc, extended exploration of each hat, detailed tension mapping, synthesis with actionable next steps. Best for complex decisions where the team is stuck in one mode.
