The actual work is tracking how the problem formulation itself evolves.\n\nAccording to ThoughtWorks: \"There is often a misconception that the Pilot program was the hard work of the innovation, and that scaling is now a case of simply turning the crank. Seeing the full scope of the Scale Up effort can expose the messiness and size of the journey ahead.\"\n\n---\n\n## 4. CONKLIN'S DIALOGUE MAPPING AND IBIS\n\n**Sources:** Conklin, J. (2005). *Dialogue Mapping: Building Shared Understanding of Wicked Problems.* Wiley; Kunz, W. & Rittel, H. (1970). IBIS original paper; Eight2Late (2021), \"Mapping project dialogues using IBIS.\"\n**URLs:** http://cognexus.org/dmforwp2.pdf | https://cognexus.org/wpf/wickedproblems.pdf | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issue-based_information_system | https://eight2late.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/10/mapping_project_dialogues_paper.pdf\n\nIBIS (Issue-Based Information System) was invented by Rittel himself as the direct response to wicked problems. It has three elements:\n- **Issues** (questions that need to be addressed)\n- **Positions** (possible answers/ideas responding to issues)\n- **Arguments** (pros and cons supporting or attacking positions)\n\nConklin identifies four properties of wicked problems relevant to dialogue:\n1. \"You don't understand the problem until you have formulated and perhaps even fielded a solution.\"\n2. \"The stakeholders have radically differing world views.\"\n3. \"The constraints change over time.\"\n4. \"The problem is never solved.\"\n\n**How structured conversation manages wickedness:** According to Conklin, \"IBIS organizes all of the issues, positions, information, and assumptions so that all participants have the issue map as a point of reference, and they can refer to it instead of trying to keep it all in their head. Thus IBIS contributes to Dialog Mapping being a force for coherence because it augments human cognition at one of its weakest points: the limits of short term memory.\"\n\n**Critical insight for the data room thesis:** Conklin describes collective intelligence as requiring tools that \"defragment\" project dynamics. The standard data room fragments by design -- it silos documents into folders. IBIS/Dialogue Mapping instead captures the ARGUMENTATIVE STRUCTURE of how decisions were made. A live data room built on this principle would show not just WHAT was decided, but WHY, by WHOM, against WHAT alternatives, with WHAT evidence.\n\n---\n\n## 5. WICKEDNESS INCREASES WITH STAKEHOLDERS\n\n**Sources:** Conklin (2005); Rittel & Webber (1973); Horn & Weber (2007).\n**URLs:** https://cognexus.org/wpf/wickedproblems.pdf | https://watereducation.org/sites/main/files/file-attachments/jeff.pdf\n\nConklin defines \"social complexity\" as the core amplifier of wickedness: \"the number and diversity of stakeholders whose participation is essential.\"\n\nFrom the Water Education Foundation presentation, Conklin maps the escalation:\n- **Technical complexity** = hard but solvable (tame)\n- **Conceptual complexity** = requires synthesis (messy)\n- **Social complexity** = gridlock between stakeholders with conflicting framings (wicked)\n\nApplied to a venture's stakeholder set:\n- **Founder** sees a technology-market opportunity\n- **VC** sees a risk-return calculation on a portfolio thesis\n- **Regulator** sees a compliance/safety question\n- **Customer** sees a pain point to be solved\n- **Employee** sees a career trajectory and equity stake\n- **Strategic partner** sees a channel or integration opportunity\n\nEach stakeholder literally perceives a DIFFERENT PROBLEM. Rittel's characteristic #9 applies: \"The choice of explanation determines the nature of the problem's resolution.\" When the VC asks \"What's your TAM?\" and the founder answers with a technology roadmap, they are speaking about different problems.\n\n**Data room implication:** A static data room presents one narrative (the founder's). A wicked-problem-aware data room would make the multiple stakeholder framings explicit, traceable, and navigable.\n\n---\n\n## 6. CLUMSY SOLUTIONS (Thompson & Verweij)\n\n**Sources:** Verweij, M. & Thompson, M. (eds.) (2006). *Clumsy Solutions for a Complex World: Governance, Politics and Plural Perceptions.* Palgrave Macmillan; Verweij et al. (2006), \"The Case for Clumsiness,\" *Public Administration* 84(4): 817-43; Ney (2015), \"Messy institutions for wicked problems: How to generate clumsy solutions?\" *Environment and Planning C*, 33: 1679.\n**URLs:** https://wires.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/wcc.804 | https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0263774X15614450 | https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1057/9780230277953_11\n\nThe \"clumsy solutions\" framework derives from Mary Douglas's Cultural Theory, which identifies four worldviews:\n- **Individualists** (low grid/low group) -- entrepreneurs, market-driven\n- **Egalitarians** (low grid/high group) -- cooperative, mission-driven\n- **Hierarchists** (high grid/high group) -- institutional, rule-driven\n- **Fatalists** (high grid/low group) -- passive, resigned\n\nAccording to Verweij et al. (2006): \"Clumsy solutions emerge from a new type of dialogue-based problem-solving strategy.\" An \"elegant\" solution satisfies only one worldview; a \"clumsy\" solution accommodates multiple, conflicting perspectives simultaneously.\n\nNey (2015) explored 17 decision-making processes and identified six most likely to generate clumsy solutions. All six required \"post-normal dialogue\" -- structured conversation that holds multiple framings in tension rather than collapsing to one.\n\n**Venture application:** A venture's strategy IS a clumsy solution. The pitch deck satisfies the VC's individualist framing (returns); the product roadmap satisfies the customer's egalitarian framing (solving real problems); the compliance documentation satisfies the regulator's hierarchist framing (rules followed). A good venture holds all of these in tension simultaneously. A data room that only shows one framing (the investor framing) is an \"elegant\" solution to a wicked problem -- and therefore the wrong tool.\n\n---\n\n## 7. HORN'S SOCIAL MESSES FRAMEWORK\n\n**Sources:** Horn, R.E. (2001). \"Knowledge Mapping for Complex Social Messes.\" Packard Foundation presentation; Horn, R.E. & Weber, R.P. (2007). \"New Tools For Resolving Wicked Problems: Mess Mapping and Resolution Mapping Processes.\"\n**URLs:** https://faculty.washington.edu/farkas/TC510-Fall2011/Horn-SocialMesses.pdf | https://complexitylabs.io/New_Tools_For_Resolving_Wicked_Problems.pdf | https://nautilus.org/gps/solving/social-messes-robert-e-horn/\n\nHorn (Stanford, H-STAR Institute) coined \"Social Messes\" after Russell Ackoff's original \"messes\" concept. According to Horn: \"Social messes are more than complicated and complex. They are ambiguous... bounded by great constraints and tightly interconnected, economically, socially, politically, technologically... seen differently from different points of view, and quite different worldviews... contain many value conflicts... are often a-logical or illogical.\"\n\nHorn's methodological contribution is the **Mess Map** -- a visual \"cross-boundary causality map\" that:\n- Places situations, events, and phenomena in \"sectors\" (deliberately drawn as blobs, not boxes, because \"boxes gave the illusion of too much neatness and not enough mess\")\n- Connects them with arrows representing causes or influences\n- Uses informal language (\"case workers are leaving in droves\") rather than bureaucratic language\n- Embeds problems as highlighted elements within sectors, forming \"a kind of embedded executive summary of the interlinked set of problems\"\n\nHorn explicitly states: \"We say resolution rather than solution. Like most important problems, Social Messes exist in a dynamic, changing world. Thus, ameliorative efforts may have substantial, yet impermanent effects.\"\n\nHorn & Weber (2007) identify that social messes are \"systems of systems\" and that this interconnection is precisely what \"makes Social Messes so resistant to analysis and, more importantly, to resolution.\"\n\n**Data room implication:** A Mess Map of a venture would show the interconnections between market dynamics, technology risk, regulatory environment, competitive landscape, team capability, and capital structure -- not as separate folders, but as a causally interlinked network. This is a knowledge graph, not a file system.\n\n---\n\n## 8. KNOWLEDGE GRAPHS AND NETWORK ANALYSIS FOR MAPPING WICKED PROBLEMS\n\n**Sources:** Horn & Weber (2007); NSF OKN Roadmap (2022), \"Use Case P: Compounding Disasters/Events (a.k.a. Wicked Problems Institute)\"; Tufts CS (2023), \"Knowledge Graphs in Practice\"; MERLTech (2025), knowledge graph applications.\n**URLs:** https://faculty.washington.edu/farkas/TC510-Fall2011/Horn-SocialMesses.pdf | https://nsf-gov-resources.nsf.gov/2022-09/OKN%20Roadmap%20-%20Appendix%20A_v03.pdf | https://www.cs.tufts.edu/~remco/publications/2023/TVCG2023-KnowledgeGraph.pdf | https://merltech.org/knowledge-graph-tech-and-real-world-applications/\n\nThe NSF's Open Knowledge Network Roadmap (2022) explicitly includes \"Use Case P -- Compounding Disasters / Events (a.k.a. Wicked Problems Institute)\" as a knowledge graph application: it \"identifies sources of supply-chain disruption that users in government and industry must respond to.\"\n\nFrom the Tufts 2023 study on knowledge graphs in practice, a practitioner (P17) stated: \"We can build faster graph database systems. Is it intellectually challenging to go do that? Definitely. But is that going to push the barrier for the world to take unknowns and turn them into knowns?\" The researcher concluded: \"Too Many Hammers, Not Enough Talking: While research continues to focus on optimizing knowledge graph databases and query languages... many technological problems are already solved. Instead... computer scientists need to be open to addressing the social problems related to KGs.\"\n\nMERLTech (2025) documents how knowledge graphs help \"researchers study cities as complex systems while supporting transparency, integrating with government data tools, and updating easily as cities evolve.\" Mario Marais's presentation demonstrates how \"relationship mapping uncovers perspectives and dependencies shaping any system.\"\n\n**The graph-as-wicked-problem-map argument:**\n- Nodes = entities (stakeholders, assumptions, decisions, market signals, technologies, regulations)\n- Edges = relationships (causes, influences, contradicts, depends-on, validates, invalidates)\n- Traversal = following causal chains across the problem space\n- Temporal versioning = tracking how the problem formulation itself evolves over time\n\nThis is not about \"solving\" the wicked problem. According to Horn, you map it to achieve \"resolution\" -- which means amelioration, not elimination. A knowledge graph of a venture's problem space is the closest digital equivalent to Horn's Mess Map.\n\n---\n\n## 9. PIVOTS AS WICKED PROBLEM BEHAVIOR\n\n**Sources:** Ries, E. (2011). *The Lean Startup*; Blank, S. (2005). *The Four Steps to the Epiphany*; Blank (2023), \"Lean Meets Wicked Problems.\"\n**URLs:** https://theleanstartup.com/principles | https://steveblank.com/2023/07/25/lean-meets-wicked-problems/ | https://entrepreneurship.asu.edu/blog/2025/09/09/when-to-pivot-your-startup-and-how-to-refocus-your-strategy/\n\nEric Ries defines a pivot as \"a structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, strategy, and engine of growth.\" According to Ries: \"Startups that succeed are those that manage to iterate enough times before running out of resources.\"\n\n**This is Rittel's characteristic #1 in action.** The problem redefines itself in response to attempted solutions. A pivot is not a failure -- it is the wicked problem asserting its nature. The Build-Measure-Learn loop is an operationalized response to characteristic #4 (no immediate test) and characteristic #5 (every attempt counts).\n\nBlank (2023) explicitly connects the Lean methodology to wicked problems: the \"Wicked Canvas\" adds \"unintended consequences\" and \"sub-problems according to the different stakeholders' perspectives\" to the standard Business Model Canvas. This is an acknowledgment that the Business Model Canvas itself was a tame-problem tool being applied to a wicked-problem domain.\n\n**Data room implication:** A static data room captures the CURRENT hypothesis. When a venture pivots, the old data room becomes obsolete -- or worse, misleading. A wicked-problem-aware data room would preserve the full hypothesis genealogy: what was believed, why it was abandoned, what replaced it, and what was learned. This is the \"argumentative structure\" that Conklin describes -- the rationale graph behind the decision history.\n\n---\n\n## 10. SATISFICING (Simon) vs. OPTIMIZING\n\n**Sources:** Simon, H.A. (1957, 1978, 1982). Multiple works on bounded rationality; Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (2024), \"Bounded Rationality\"; Simon Nobel Prize lecture (1978).\n**URLs:** https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/bounded-rationality/ | https://www.nobelprize.org/uploads/2018/06/simon-lecture.pdf | https://cdn.aaai.org/Symposia/Spring/1998/SS-98-05/SS98-05-015.pdf\n\nHerbert Simon (Nobel Prize, Economics, 1978) established that human decision-makers do not optimize -- they \"satisfice,\" a term combining \"satisfy\" and \"suffice.\" According to Simon's Nobel lecture: \"Two concepts are central to the characterization: search and satisficing. If the alternatives for choice are not given initially to the decision maker, then he must search for them.\"\n\nSimon's key insight: \"In complex real-world situations, optimization becomes approximate optimization since the description of the real-world is radically simplified until reduced to a degree of complication that the decision maker can handle. Satisficing seeks simplification in a somewhat different direction, retaining more of the detail of the real-world situation, but settling for a satisfactory, rather than approximate-best, decision.\"\n\nFrom the Stanford Encyclopedia: \"Satisficing is the strategy of considering the options available to you for choice until you find one that meets or exceeds a predefined threshold -- your aspiration level.\"\n\n**Venture application:** Every venture decision is satisficing, not optimizing. There is no optimal product-market fit, no optimal cap table, no optimal go-to-market strategy. There are only satisfactory-enough decisions made under bounded rationality. The aspiration level itself is dynamic -- it changes as the venture learns.\n\n**Data room implication:** An optimizing data room would attempt to present the \"best\" version of reality. A satisficing data room would present the CURRENT state of knowledge, explicitly bounded, with aspiration levels visible and decision rationale traceable. This is the difference between a sales document and a navigation instrument.\n\n---\n\n## SYNTHESIS: THE ARGUMENT FOR THE WICKED PROBLEM MANAGEMENT TOOL\n\nThe theoretical backbone, assembled:\n\n**Premise 1:** Every deep-tech venture is a wicked problem (Rittel & Webber, 1973). It exhibits all 10 characteristics simultaneously.\n\n**Premise 2:** Current data rooms are tame-problem tools (Grint, 2005). Each stakeholder sees a different problem. A data room that presents one narrative (the founder's pitch) systematically hides the wickedness from the very people who need to understand it.\n\n**Premise 5:** Effective responses to wicked problems are \"clumsy solutions\" that accommodate multiple conflicting perspectives (Thompson & Verweij, 2006). A data room that collapses multiple framings into one \"clean narrative\" is an elegant solution -- and therefore wrong for the problem type.\n\n**Premise 6:** The problem space must be MAPPED, not solved (Horn, 2001, 2007). A Mess Map shows interconnections. A knowledge graph is the digital implementation of a Mess Map.\n\n**Premise 7:** Pivots are the wicked problem asserting its nature (Ries, 2011; Blank, 2023). A data room must preserve hypothesis genealogy, not just the current hypothesis.\n\n**Premise 8:** All venture decisions are satisficing, not optimizing (Simon, 1957-1982). The data room should make bounded rationality visible -- showing aspiration levels, search paths, and good-enough decisions rather than pretending to present optimal outcomes.\n\n**Conclusion:** A venture data room must be built as a WICKED PROBLEM MANAGEMENT TOOL -- a live, graph-structured, argumentatively-rich, multi-stakeholder, temporally-versioned navigation instrument.
