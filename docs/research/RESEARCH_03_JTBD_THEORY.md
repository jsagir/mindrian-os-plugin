CHRISTENSEN'S JTBD FRAMEWORK -- CORE PRINCIPLES\n\n**Source:** Christensen Institute (christenseninstitute.org), HBS Online, Forbes, Fullstory\n\nThe framework rests on a single reorientation: customers do not buy products -- they \"hire\" them to make progress in specific circumstances. Christensen and Bob Moesta developed the theory to explain why 75-85% of new product launches fail financially (according to Christensen's own estimate, per HBS Online).\n\n**The hiring metaphor operates on two axes:**\n- **Hiring:** A customer pulls a product into their life when a \"job\" arises -- a problem or opportunity they need to resolve\n- **Firing:** When a product fails to do the job, the customer fires it and seeks an alternative\n\n**The canonical illustration** is the milkshake study. A fast-food chain discovered morning milkshake buyers were not comparing against competing milkshakes -- they were comparing against bananas, donuts, bagels, and Snickers bars. The \"job\" was: make my commute less boring and keep me full until lunch. The milkshake's viscosity, cup-holder fit, and 20-minute consumption time made it the best hire. The competitive set was defined by the job, not the product category.\n\n**Core principles (per Christensen Institute and Learning Loop):**\n1. People buy to make progress, not to acquire products\n2. Jobs incorporate functional, social, and emotional dimensions simultaneously\n3. A job is defined by the circumstance, not the customer demographic\n4. Jobs are stable over time (the job of \"listening to music on the go\" has persisted across vinyl, cassettes, CDs, MP3, and streaming)\n5. Products that become tightly associated with a specific job become \"purpose brands\" (e.g., FedEx = \"send this with perfect certainty as fast as possible\")\n6. Focus creates differentiation -- products marketed against a specific job command premium pricing\n\n**Source URLs:**\n- https://www.christenseninstitute.org/theory/jobs-to-be-done/\n- https://online.hbs.edu/blog/post/3-keys-to-understanding-jobs-to-be-done\n- https://www.forbes.com/sites/deniselyohn/2020/02/04/clay-christensens-jobs-to-be-done-innovation-theory-challenges-common-innovation-and-marketing-methods/\n- https://www.fullstory.com/blog/clayton-christensen-jobs-to-be-done-framework-product-development/\n\n---\n\n### 2. ULWICK'S OUTCOME-DRIVEN INNOVATION (ODI)\n\n**Source:** anthonyulwick.com, strategyn.com, Wikipedia (ODI entry), Strategyn whitepaper (PDF)\n\nTony Ulwick operationalized JTBD into a quantitative innovation process starting in 1991. He introduced Christensen to the concept (per Strategyn's published history) and built the methodology that turns jobs into measurable inputs. ODI claims an 86% success rate versus the industry average of ~17%.\n\n**The Desired Outcome Statement structure:**\n\nEach job step has associated \"desired outcomes\" -- structured as measurable statements following a specific syntax:\n> **[Direction of improvement] + [unit of measure] + [object of control] + [contextual clarifier]**\n\nExample: \"Minimize the time it takes to verify the cut is on track.\" These are not features or solutions -- they are customer-defined performance metrics.\n\nFor any given job, Ulwick's research shows customers have **50-150 distinct desired outcomes**. This map is the backbone for exhaustive needs capture.\n\n**The Opportunity Algorithm:**\n\n> **Opportunity Score = Importance + max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)**\n\nCustomers rate each outcome on a 1-10 scale for both importance and current satisfaction. The formula double-weights importance relative to satisfaction. An opportunity score exceeding 10.0 indicates a significant value-creation opportunity. This is the quantitative engine for feature prioritization.\n\n**Source URLs:**\n- https://anthonyulwick.com/jobs-to-be-done/\n- https://anthonyulwick.com/outcome-driven-innovation/\n- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outcome-Driven_Innovation\n- https://innovationroundtable.com/summit/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Strategyn_what_is_Outcome_Driven_Innovation.pdf\n\n---\n\n### 3. FUNCTIONAL, EMOTIONAL, AND SOCIAL JOBS\n\n**Source:** Christensen Institute, UXtweak, Designative.info, Adience (B2B-specific)\n\nAll jobs incorporate three dimensions simultaneously. These are not separate jobs -- they are layers of the same job.\n\n**Functional jobs:** The utilitarian task the customer needs to accomplish. This is the anchor. Example: \"Get from point A to point B.\" The functional job defines the market and scope of innovation.\n\n**Emotional jobs:** How the customer wants to feel (or avoid feeling) while executing the job. These split into:\n- **Personal emotional jobs:** \"Feel confident I'm not being price-gouged\" (Lyft example, per Todd/Designative)\n- **Desire for control/security:** \"Feel relaxed that I don't need to deal with traffic\"\n\n**Social jobs:** How the customer wants to be perceived by others during or after the job. Example: Adult diapers have a social job of \"avoid embarrassment in public.\" A smart door lock has a social job of \"be seen as an innovator.\"\n\n**B2B/Venture application (per Adience):**\n\nIn B2B contexts, the three job types map differently across roles:\n- **Users** hire for functional jobs (perform the core task efficiently)\n- **Buyers/procurement** hire for financial/risk-management jobs\n- **Executives** hire for emotional jobs (confidence, career protection) and social jobs (organizational reputation)\n\nThe B2B JTBD analysis must identify three distinct customer types per Ulwick's framework:\n1. **The job executor** -- the person performing the job\n2. **The purchase decision maker** -- the buyer\n3. **The product lifecycle support team** -- those who install, maintain, service\n\nEach has different jobs with different desired outcomes.
